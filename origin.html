On board the USS Ronald Reagan during military exercise Talisman Sabre

ABC Capricornia
By Megan Hendry

Posted July 22, 2019 19:56:50

Video: A snippet of life on board the USS Ronald Reagan during Talisman Sabre 2019(ABC News)

"Here we go, here we go, here we go!"

The aircrewman yells and throws his fist in the air three times.

That's the signal, we're about to land. I brace for the force.

For the last hour and a half, I've been strapped into the back of a US Navy C-2 Greyhound plane in a four-point harness, wearing a flight deck helmet, goggles and what they call a 'horse collar'.

It's dark. There are only two small windows in the bare-metal cabin.

I'm facing the rear of the plane, my feet are on the cargo ramp. You can't buy this kind of leg room.

The plane's pilot, Lieutenant Commander Andrew Dumm brings us in for a text-book arrested landing on the flight deck of US Navy aircraft carrier, USS Ronald Reagan.

Photo: Lieutenant Commander Andrew Dumm has been flying the US Navy C-2 Greyhound plane for 11 years.(ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry)

The force pushes me back hard into my seat as the plane goes from about 200 kilometres an hour to a standstill in two seconds.

Sitting next to me is the plane's Chief Crewman, Kyle Bell.

He's originally from Columbia, Missouri but is now based in Japan with the Navy's air wing.

"I was attracted to the discipline, the travel and for the college education," he says about his decision to enlist in the US Navy straight out of high school.

He's studying a business degree and sees a future for himself in Navy logistics.

Photo: Chief Crewman Kyle Bell is one of 34,000 troops participating in the military exercise Talisman Sabre 2019.(ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry)

The plane he crews is one of about 60 fixed wing and rotary aircraft in the USS Ronald Reagan's armament.

The 333-metre long ship has 18 decks and is one of 11 of its kind in the US Navy fleet.

About 5,000 people live on board at one time, with about 18,000 meals served up daily in the mess hall and ward room.

The ship and its company are somewhere in the Coral Sea, about 350 nautical miles from Rockhampton.

The exact location is classified.

Photo: An F/A-18 fighter jet prepares for take-off during defence exercise Talisman Sabre 2019.(ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry )

It's part of exercise Talisman Sabre, the largest joint military exercise ever held between Australia and the United States.

The exercise involves more than 34,000 military personnel and for the first time also includes allied troops from Japan, New Zealand and Great Britain.

Rear Admiral Karl Thomas is the Commander of Taskforce 70, which is the Battle Force of the 7th Fleet and includes the USS Ronald Reagan.

He's served 33 years in the US Navy but this is his first Talisman Sabre exercise.

Photo: The ship and its company are somewhere in the Coral Sea, about 350 nautical miles from Rockhampton.(ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry)

"It's been a great exercise for us being able to operate with our closest allies," he says.

"It's the type of training we need to do to further hone our warfighting skills."

Every day of the exercise up to 100 fighter jets, electronic attack, cargo and surveillance aircraft take off from the ship's flight deck.

"We provide maritime superiority and air superiority so that the amphibious phase can happen," Read Admiral Thomas says.

"Today we had some strikes go launch against some land targets and maritime targets that are playing the bad guy."

Photo: Catapult Officer Lieutenant Sarah Klenke stands on the flight deck of the USS Ronald Reagan. (ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry)

The USS Ronald Reagan is forward deployed in Japan at Yokosuka, while the airwing contingent is based in Atsugi and Iwakuni.

"Clearly it's one of the most capable ships in this region," Read Admiral Thomas says.

"The fact that we can move quickly from one exercise to another allows us to cover a lot of ground and work with a lot of partners and allies and increase the strength and resolve of the US presence in the area."

The Nimitz-class aircraft carrier has a 63-bed hospital, six gyms, a chapel, dentist and even a Starbucks, albeit make-shift.

Commissioned in 2003, the USS Ronald Reagan is now in its 17th year of service, and while the first of the new, Ford-class aircraft carrier has now been commissioned, Rear Admiral Thomas says the Nimitz will remain relevant.

"The thing that makes an aircraft carrier modern is the airwing," he says.

Photo: Small, coloured thumb tacks are pinned into the tiny planes, denoting everything from take-off order to weapons uploads. (ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry)

Up in the flight deck control room, technology is still catching up.

For the next year or so, officers will continue to use a physical scaled model to track the movements of aircraft on and off the ship. It's also known as the 'Ouija board'.

Flight deck Leading Petty Officer Christopher Gibson says it's the "nervous centre" of the ship.

"Everything that happens outside on the flight deck, happens here on the board," he says.

Small, coloured thumb tacks are pinned into the tiny planes, denoting everything from take-off order to weapons uploads.

He says the system will become digital in about a year, meaning the control room will become a classified area and off-limits to visitors.

Photo: USS Ronald Reagan has about 60 fixed wing and rotary aircraft in its armament.(ABC Capricornia: Megan Hendry)

As the tiny planes move around the board, the flight deck crew outside move with purpose and precision.

Catapult officers — or shooters — guide the pilots onto a sling-shot style mechanism in preparation for take-off.

No-one speaks, they just use hand signals.

It's far too loud to hear anything other than the roar of jet engines.

When it's time for take off, the jet is connected to a holdback fitting on a trail bar on the deck of the carrier via a wire rope.

It moves forward slowly, placing tension on the holdback fitting as the pilot thrusts the engines.

At a pre-set tension, the holdback fitting breaks, and the taut rope catapults the aircraft along the 100 metre runway off the edge of the carrier and into the sky.

The pilots complete their mission and return to the ship, landing in another spectacular display of military precision.

Exercise Talisman Sabre finishes on Wednesday.
