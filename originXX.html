What happened in the crucial ten minutes before Trump stopped war with Iran?

Updated June 22, 2019 18:41:23

Photo: Talk has turned away from war towards sanctions against Iran. (AP: Jacquelyn Martin)

The planes were ready their deadly cargo poised for delivery within a half-hour.

Key points:

  • Donald Trump defied key advisors, instead apparently listening to Fox News presenter Tucker Carlson
  • The President said possible deaths of 150 Iranians shaped his decision not to strike against the Islamic Republic
  • Senior Democrats have given rare praise for Mr Trump for his decision to back down

It was the latest among a string of increasingly serious incidents in the Gulf region, a critical artery for global oil supplies, since mid-May.

Senior military advisers zeroed in on a plan to launch strikes on a trio of sites within Iran, and it was up to Mr Trump to give the final go-ahead.

If the planes took off, Mr Trump later recounted to NBC, they would soon be at "a point where you wouldn't turn back or couldn't turn back."

Video: The Iranian military released this video claiming to show the drone being shot down.(ABC News)

How would he make his decision? "My gut," he told legislators.

When the military officers came looking for the President's final go-ahead, Mr Trump said he had one last question.

"'I want to know something before you go,'" Mr Trump recounted.

"'How many people will be killed?'"

Photo: Iran shooting down a US drone was the first time it has directly attacked the American military amid tensions over the nuclear deal. (Office of the Iranian Supreme Leader via AP)

'The President was really wrestling with it'

Hours earlier, reporters had asked the President about how he would respond to Tehran.

"You'll find out. You'll find out," Mr Trump said.

"They made a very big mistake."

A year ago, Mr Trump defied most of the United States' allies by pulling out of the Iran nuclear deal and strengthening sanctions on the regime, choking the Iranian economy and pushing Tehran to escalate tensions.

A briefing on Iran on Thursday brought together top congressional leaders, national security committee chairmen, and the heads of the House and Senate foreign relations committees.

Photo: Ending conflicts in the Middle East was one of the promises Trump made on the campaign trail in 2016. (AP: Jacquelyn Martin)

Once assembled, the politicians around the table made their case, one by one.

Mr Trump seemed eager to hear their opinions, even those of House Democrats who have launched a slew of investigations into the President.

Trump trying to stall as tensions with Iran near tipping point


It's possible the President is buying time publicly as he dukes it out with advisers backstage, writes Zoe Daniel.

The legislators saw "a commander in chief who struggled with the issue," said Republican Senator Jim Risch of Idaho, the chairman of the Foreign Relations Committee.

"It was painful for him."

Democrats made the case for caution, for partnering with allies, for taking a breath to de-escalate.

"These conflicts have a way of escalating," Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer told the President.

Even if Mr Trump didn't intend to go to war, Mr Schumer said, he could "bumble" into one.

Photo: Senior Democrats have applauded Mr Trump's decision not to strike at Iran. (AP: J. Scott Applewhite)

"The President was really wrestling with it," House Armed Services Chairman Adam Smith, a Democrat, was quoted by CNN as saying.

War was 'only 10 minutes away'

External Link:Last night Fox News Tucker Carlson had a segment

Televisions across the White House were tuned, as usual, to Fox News.

Tucker Carlson's image flickered on the screen as he made his case earlier in the week against going to war with Iran.

Mr Carlson was making a similar case to Mr Trump in private, according to a White House official and a Republican close to the West Wing.

Mr Trump had been soliciting a wide array of opinions about Iran after a pair of tankers were damaged a week earlier near the Strait of Hormuz, an incident US officials blamed on the Iranians.

The President was growing frustrated with his national security adviser John Bolton's advocacy for a strike, the officials said.

The attack on the drone put the military option on the table.

Photo: The US Department of Defence released the map on the left showing where they say the drone was shot down. Iran's Foreign Minister tweeted the map on the right.(AP/Twitter)

But when Mr Trump asked his question on Thursday about how many Iranians could die in the strikes, the answer gave him pause.

He was told 150 Iranian lives were at stake.

"I thought about it for a second," Mr Trump told NBC, "and I said: 'You know what? They shot down an unmanned drone, plane, whatever you want to call it."

"And here we are sitting with 150 dead people that would have taken place probably within a half an hour after I said go ahead.' And I didn't like it I didn't think it was proportionate."

Photo: Asked how he made his decision against going to war with Iran, Trump said "my gut".(AP: Evan Vucci)

Attacks on oil tankers have ratcheted up tensions

Mr Trump's decision to cancel what he said was a planned attack on three sites suggests he wants a diplomatic solution to end weeks of festering tensions with Iran, which Washington accuses of a spate of attacks on oil tankers in the Gulf region.

Oil futures rose more than 1 per cent to above $US65 ($94) per barrel on Friday over worries about possible disruptions to crude exports from the Gulf.

There have also been explosions on six oil tankers that the US suspects Iran or its proxies of carrying out. Iran has denied any involvement.

US forces are preparing to evacuate nearly 400 people working for Lockheed Martin Corp and Sallyport Global from an Iraqi military base north of Baghdad over potential security threats, Iraqi military sources said on Friday.

The sources did not give any details about the threats.

Photo: Iran has denied any involvement in attacks on oil tankers in the Gulf. (US Department of Defense via AP)

Three bases hosting US forces in Iraq have been attacked in the past week with no claims of responsibility.

Local officials blamed Iran-backed Shiite militias for one of the incidents.

On Friday, Mr Trump spoke with Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman about Middle East stability and the oil market, the White House said.

German Chancellor Angela Merkel said she and fellow EU leaders "are concerned about the situation and support diplomatic negotiations, a political solution for a very tense situation".

Some global airlines are re-routing flights to avoid Iran-controlled airspace over the Strait of Hormuz and Gulf of Oman after the US Federal Aviation Administration barred US air carriers from the area.

Photo: National Security Adviser John Bolton has advocated a "maximum pressure" campaign on Iran. (AP: Evan Vucci)

Decision has drawn mixed responses in Washington

The President, long opposed to being drawn into a military conflict in the Middle East and in particular with an unpredictable foe like Iran, played up the drama of the moment.

External Link:Proportionate to shooting down an unmanned drone

Mr Trump tweeted on Friday morning that the military had been "cocked and loaded" and that the weaponry was only 10 minutes away from being deployed.

Mr Bolton, Secretary of State Mike Pompeo and CIA Director Gina Haspel, along with the rest of Mr Trump's team, favoured a retaliatory strike, a senior Trump administration official said.

"There was complete unanimity amongst the President's advisers and DOD (Department of Defence) leadership on an appropriate response to Iran's activities," said the official.

"The President made the final decision."

Mr Trump's decision drew mixed reviews in Washington, with some people criticising him for flinching while others, notably senior Democrats, praised what they saw as restraint.

"A strike of that amount of collateral damage would be very provocative, and I'm glad the President did not take that," House Speaker Nancy Pelosi, the top Democrat in Congress, told reporters.

However, Michael Makovsky, a former Pentagon official who heads the Jewish Institute for National Security of America, a think tank that favours strong US-Israeli security ties, said Trump was undermining US credibility.

"Trump has given the impression he lost his nerve," Mr Makovsky said in a statement.

On Friday afternoon, members of Congress filtered into the White House south lawn for the annual congressional picnic just steps away from the windowless, basement Situation Room where security officials had debated what could come next.

Photo: Iranian-American protesters have called for regime change in Iran, but oppose war. (AP: Alex Brandon)

Senator Risch's prediction: "There's going to be something more proportional, obviously, and I suspect it's going to be not kinetic action."
